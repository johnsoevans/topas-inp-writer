# Algorithm Papers — Summaries

This file replaces bundled copies of the algorithm papers that used to live in `references/papers/`. Each entry below is an original summary, written from reading the paper, describing the problem it addresses and the method it introduces — not a reproduction of the paper's own text, figures, or equations. Citations are given so the underlying paper can be looked up directly if exact wording, derivations, or data are needed.

(An earlier version of this skill kept six of these papers as full PDFs, reasoning that their IUCr cover page permits the author to self-host that exact reprint. On reflection, that grant is scoped to hosting on the author's own site/institutional repository, not to bundling copies into a file that gets actively distributed to other people — so those six are now summarized here too, and no PDFs are bundled in this skill at all.)

## Fundamental parameters and peak-shape modeling

**A Fundamental Parameters Approach to X-ray Line-Profile Fitting** — Cheary, R.W. & Coelho, A.A. (1992). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 25, 109–121.
Introduces the fundamental-parameters (FP) approach that underlies TOPAS's peak generation: rather than fitting a peak with an empirical analytical function (Pseudo-Voigt, Pearson VII, etc.), the peak shape is synthesized directly from the emission profile, the diffractometer's physical dimensions, and the specimen's physical properties, all convoluted together. This makes refined parameters (slit sizes, source size, sample transparency, etc.) physically meaningful rather than empirical fit coefficients. This is the foundational paper behind the FP peak-generation machinery described in the manual's Peak Generation chapter. States an explicit sanity check: refined instrumental parameters should land within ±10% of their directly-measured values — a physically-unrealistic refined value signals a genuine model deficiency, not just noise. Also documents two specific correlation traps worth checking when an FP refinement won't stabilize: target (source) width and receiving-slit width are nearly degenerate (same profile shape, independent of 2θ) so only one should be refined at a time, and slit-length vs. flat-specimen-error functions are similarly correlated. The paper's convolution-interpolation scheme (vs. naive direct convolution) is ~100x faster, and shows that the naive histogram-convolution approach's fitted 2θ values are spuriously dependent on the arbitrary calculation step size — the interpolation approach removes that dependency, which is part of the motivation for TOPAS's own `Peak_Calculation_Step`/`convolution_step` machinery.

**Synthesizing and Fitting Linear Position-Sensitive Detector Step-Scanned Line Profiles** — Cheary, R.W. & Coelho, A.A. (1994). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 27, 673–681.
Extends the FP approach to instruments using a linear position-sensitive detector (LPSD) with step-scanning. Derives the theoretical contributions of defocusing, parallax error, thermal-noise broadening, and detector pixel size to the line shape, and shows these can be convoluted with the emission profile to synthesize an accurate profile for step-scanned LPSD data. Reports a concrete speed/resolution tradeoff behind the `lpsd_th2_angular_range_degrees` keyword: LPSD step-scanning is ~100x faster to collect than conventional receiving-slit scanning for equal counting statistics, but summing over a full 10°2θ detector window broadens peaks by ~0.06°2θ versus conventional; narrowing the summation window to ~3° nearly matches conventional resolution while remaining ~10x faster. The three LPSD-specific parameters (divergence angle, detector depth, summation window) are strongly correlated — reliable simultaneous refinement of all three needs a wide 2θ range including low-angle lines; otherwise fix two and refine only the divergence angle.

**Axial Divergence in a Conventional X-ray Powder Diffractometer. I. Theoretical Foundations** — Cheary, R.W. & Coelho, A.A. (1998). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 31, 851–861.
Develops a general theoretical model for the axial-divergence aberration in conventional lab diffractometers equipped with incident- and/or diffracted-beam Soller slits, deriving the aberration profile directly from instrument dimensions (source, sample and receiving-slit axial lengths, Soller slit angular apertures) in three progressively more general stages. Motivated by a specific failure of the older simple line-source model: it incorrectly predicts axial broadening/asymmetry passes through exactly zero at 2θ=90°, when in reality it never fully vanishes and instead passes through a minimum near 2θ≈120° (due to incident-beam axial divergence) — this new model corrects that. The refinable quantities are the axial lengths of source/sample/receiving-slit and the primary/secondary Soller angular apertures; in practice specimen-dependent axial narrowing (e.g. from an oriented thin film) can get absorbed into one of these refining away from its true physical value, which is worth checking for if a refined axial length looks implausible.

**Axial Divergence in a Conventional X-ray Powder Diffractometer. II. Realization and Evaluation in a Fundamental-Parameter Profile Fitting Procedure** — Cheary, R.W. & Coelho, A.A. (1998). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 31, 862–868.
The companion paper to Part I: implements the axial-divergence model within an FP profile-fitting and Rietveld program, using a direct convolution-interpolation procedure to keep the computation fast, and validates it against real data from two different diffractometers across a range of axial-divergence conditions. Because the five axial parameters are highly correlated, the paper recommends refining only one (usually a Soller aperture angle) plus possibly one other (e.g. receiving-slit width) — refining all five at once destabilizes the fit. Reports concrete accuracy payoff versus the older parallel-incident-beam model: Rwp improved from 10.1% to 7.2% for LaB6 and 7.8% to 6.4% for CeO2. Also notes a graphite post-monochromator behaves like an effective diffracted-beam Soller slit of ~5–6° equivalent aperture. Numerically, the aberration function's singularities are handled by convolving with an impulse function whose width equals the data step size, letting only ~30 angular-summation steps reproduce an accurate profile — versus 1000+ steps for brute-force summation, the same aberration-buffering idea used elsewhere in TOPAS's peak generation.

**A capillary specimen aberration for describing X-ray powder diffraction line profiles for convergent, divergent and parallel beam geometries** — Coelho, A.A. & Rowles, M.R. (2017). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 50, 1519–1526.
Describes a fast numerical procedure for the peak-shape and position aberrations specific to cylindrical capillary specimens (as opposed to flat-plate specimens), across convergent, divergent, and parallel-beam geometries. Uses TOPAS's aberration-averaging technique so that even a diffraction pattern with thousands of peaks only needs on the order of 30 disc-aberration calculations (Ny=40 discs, chosen for peak-shape/position accuracy, not just intensity accuracy), keeping the approach computationally practical within a full Rietveld refinement. The refined `packing_density` parameter (see `capillary_u_cm_inv`) is physically the ratio of the sample's actual packing density to a fully-dense capillary — it multiplies the theoretical linear attenuation coefficient to account for real (sub-theoretical) powder packing, and refined to a physically sensible ~0.31 in the paper's LaB6 example. States a specific correlation caveat: the perpendicular/parallel displacement-correction parameters are strongly correlated with the lattice parameter (up to 90% correlation reported) — for a well-aligned instrument these should be fixed to zero rather than refined, otherwise the refined lattice parameters become unreliable.

## Minimization and computational performance

**A bound constrained conjugate gradient solution method as applied to crystallographic refinement problems** — Coelho, A.A. (2005). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 38, 455–461.
Describes modifications to the conjugate-gradient method (for solving the symmetric positive-definite normal-equations system that arises each refinement iteration) that give roughly an 84x speed-up over LU decomposition for a dense system of 1325 unknowns, with a further gain when upper/lower parameter-bound constraints are applied. This underlies TOPAS's ability to refine large numbers of parameters efficiently, including in Pawley, rigid-body, and general structure refinement. The striking underlying reason: the number of CG iterations needed converges to around ten *regardless* of the number of parameters or whether the matrix is dense, sparse, or ill-conditioned — this near-constant iteration count, not just per-iteration cost, is what drives the speed-up. States a specific mechanism limitation directly relevant to `min`/`max` vs. `restraint`/`penalty` keyword choice: bound constraints (BCCG's `min`/`max`) only work when the constrained quantity *is itself* one of the refined parameters (e.g. a rigid-body/z-matrix bond distance defined as its own parameter); if a distance is instead defined implicitly via six independent atomic-coordinate parameters, BCCG's bounding mechanism can't enforce it directly, and a `restraint` (which modifies the A matrix instead) is needed to constrain it.

**Whole-profile structure solution from powder diffraction data using simulated annealing** — Coelho, A.A. (2000). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 33, 899–908.
Develops and systematically characterizes the simulated-annealing control parameters used for structure solution directly against the whole diffraction profile (rather than extracted intensities), starting only from space group and lattice parameters. Notably folds electrostatic-potential penalty functions (via a general real-space summation valid for any space group) directly into the non-linear least-squares Rietveld procedure, and introduces a general weighting scheme for penalty functions that avoids needing to tune weights by hand. A significant departure from textbook SA worth knowing when reasoning about TOPAS's `continue_after_convergence`/`temperature` behavior: this implementation drops the classic Metropolis accept/reject test entirely — every new configuration is accepted regardless of the change in the objective function, relying instead on the minimization procedure itself to recover the global minimum after a random perturbation (shown to work reliably for single-atom-swap-scale perturbations). Because penalties operate in real space, resolution is effectively capped around ~1.1 Å (the size of the smallest atom typically being placed). Reports empirically-tuned defaults: penalty weight K1=2, and a temperature window of 0.6<T<0.9 (a constant T=0.7 is a reasonable default, giving ~70-90% of moves accepted as "downhill"). A practical tip stated in the paper: Rwp calculated with penalty functions still included runs ~2-3% higher than without them, so the recommendation is to drop penalties for the final refinement pass once a solution is found.

## Structure solution — charge flipping and band flipping

**A charge-flipping algorithm incorporating the tangent formula for solving difficult structures** — Coelho, A.A. (2007). *Acta Cryst.* A63, 400–406.
Extends the charge-flipping method of Oszlányi & Sütő (2004) by folding the direct-methods tangent formula into the iterative process, which acts as a corrective influence that lets the algorithm solve structures at resolutions worse than 1 Å — turning cases that previously took days (or failed) into ones solved in minutes. Also introduces flipping a percentage of charge (rather than charge below a fixed threshold) and dampening high-charge regions to avoid the algorithm converging on a spurious "one or two intense peaks" (uranium-atom) solution. Concrete default control values: 60% of charge flipped by default, a damping factor β=1/2 (dampens strong density into a Gaussian √2x broader in FWHM), 30 triplets per Eh for good-resolution data (up to 200 for poor-resolution data), and 50% of reflections treated as weak. On simulated 1 Å data, the tangent-formula version solved 100/100 test runs averaging 478–585 iterations; without the tangent-formula modification, neither of two comparable test structures solved in 200,000 iterations — a striking illustration of how much the tangent-formula addition matters for the poor-resolution/powder-diffraction case that TOPAS's charge-flipping keywords are commonly used for (see `references/22-charge-flipping.md`).

**Asymmetric band-flipping for time-of-flight neutron diffraction data** — Whitfield, P.S. & Coelho, A.A. (2016). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 49, 1806–1809.
Adapts charge flipping for neutron time-of-flight data, where the usual assumption that negative scattering density is unphysical breaks down because some elements have negative neutron scattering lengths. Band-flipping applies both a positive and a negative threshold during flipping (rather than a single positive one), combined with low-density elimination, and the paper reports testing this against real powder (Mn(TCNE)2) and Laue single-crystal (scolecite) TOF neutron data. This is the paper behind TOPAS's `flip_neutron` keyword, which sets the asymmetric negative-density threshold multiplier and forces use of neutron scattering lengths — optimum values found were 0.52 for the lower-resolution powder case and 0.35 for the higher-resolution Laue single-crystal case, illustrating that lower-quality/lower-resolution data needs a larger negative threshold. Band-flipping ran at ~60 iterations/second on a dual-core laptop in the paper's tests, fast enough to be useful for on-the-fly data-quality screening during collection, not just final structure solution. (Two versions of this manuscript exist in the source material — `band-flipping.pdf` is a pre-typesetting author draft and `charge-flipping-neutron-aj5281.pdf` is the final IUCr-typeset version above; they are the same paper, not two different ones. An earlier version of this summary mis-cited this as "Acta Cryst. A72" — there is no such published paper; the correct citation is the J. Appl. Cryst. one above.)

## Indexing

**Indexing of powder diffraction patterns by iterative use of singular value decomposition** — Coelho, A.A. (2003). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 36, 86–95.
Introduces a fast indexing method effective on lattices of all symmetries, using singular value decomposition (SVD) iteratively to solve the linear equations relating hkl indices to d-spacings, wrapped in a Monte Carlo search of parameter space. Reported as outperforming ITO, DICVOL91, and TREOR90 on simulated data, and notably more tolerant of noisy data, missing high-d-spacing reflections, and large diffractometer zero errors than the alternatives compared. Zero errors up to 0.05°2θ and d-spacing errors up to 0.03°2θ don't significantly hurt the success rate; beyond that, solution uniqueness becomes ambiguous. Monoclinic lattices are searched using all six unique space-group hkl sets (P2, P21, Pc, P21/c, C2, Cc), not just the Bravais lattice. The reflection weighting function uses d_o^m|Δ2θ|I_o with an optimal fixed exponent m≈4, but the paper found that *randomizing* m between 0–6 each iteration actually performs better than any fixed m — a "controlled randomness helps" result relevant to the `index_max_th2_error`/weighting behavior described in `references/14-indexing.md`.

**An indexing algorithm independent of peak position extraction for X-ray powder diffraction patterns** — Coelho, A.A. (2017). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 50, 1323–1330.
Introduces Lp-Search, an indexing algorithm that sidesteps the usual requirement of first extracting discrete peak positions (which is unreliable in heavily overlapped patterns) — instead combining a Monte Carlo search of lattice-parameter space with a Pawley refinement at the end of each trial, guided by a new figure-of-merit function G. Reported to index monoclinic-to-cubic lattices in seconds and triclinic lattices within a minute or two (concrete examples: PbSO4 <2s, monoclinic cimetidine ~10s, triclinic AlVO4 ~40s — all distributed as TOPAS test datasets), including on patterns missing a significant number of the highest-d-spacing peaks. G evaluates at ~300,000 evaluations/sec on a quad-core machine (threaded), and is used directly as a penalty function in the first few Pawley-refinement iterations of each trial. High-intensity impurity peaks raise the minimum G value at the correct solution but don't eliminate it as the minimum; low-intensity impurities are tolerated without special handling.

## Stacking faults

**Averaging the intensity of many-layered structures for accurate stacking-fault analysis using Rietveld refinement** — Coelho, A.A., Evans, J.S.O. & Lewis, J.W. (2016). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 49, 1740–1749.
Describes the speed-ups that make Rietveld refinement of stacking-faulted supercells (which can contain hundreds of thousands of atoms and reflections) computationally practical: a peaks buffer approximating huge numbers of hkl-dependent peak shapes with a few hundred calculated peaks, an efficient averaging process across many large supercells, a smoothing technique letting small supercells stand in for supercells 10–20x larger, and efficient stacking-sequence generation algorithms. This is the paper behind the manual's Stacking Faults chapter and the `layer`/`stack`/`generate_stack_sequences` keywords. Reports an ~10^4x overall speed-up, with a concrete real-world example (La2O2Cu0.667Cd0.667Se2): a full Rwp-surface calculation dropped from ~15 hours (traditional approach: one huge supercell, 100 repeats) to 160 seconds using the new averaging. The key equivalence behind the smoothing trick: it's the *product* N_str x N_v (number of averaged structures times layers-per-structure) that determines faulting statistics, so a small supercell (N_v=200) averaged over many trial structures with smoothing can stand in for a huge supercell (N_v=5000) without smoothing, at ~10x the speed. `layers_tol` (called q in the paper) defaults to 0.5 for the stacking-sequence-generation termination constraint, shown to give a narrower/better-defined faulting-probability distribution than an unconstrained q=1. Notably, stacking-vector coordinates themselves are typically *not* independently refined — the data can't support thousands of such parameters — instead only a handful of layer-thickness parameters are refined, a useful caveat against over-parameterizing a stacking model.

## PDF (pair distribution function) methods

**Fast synthesis and refinement of the atomic pair distribution function** — Coelho, A.A., Chater, P.A. & Kern, A. (2015). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 48, 869–875.
The speed problem: a real G(r) for a complex material out to r_max = 50 Å typically comprises hundreds of thousands to millions of Gaussians (one per atomic pair), each with a potentially unique FWHM (from its pair's isotropic displacement parameters) — synthesizing and refining against this directly would be far too slow for iterative least squares. The fix: group pairs whose Gaussians have similar-enough FWHM (within a ratio R_min ≤ f_p/f_a ≤ 1.0, empirically R_min=0.6 keeps the fit error under 0.2%) and approximate each group with a single shared narrower-FWHM Gaussian f_p. Within a group, each pair contributes a delta function ("stick") split between its two nearest r grid points; all sticks for the group are laid down into one stick pattern, which is then convoluted *once* with the shared Gaussian to produce that group's partial G(r) — collapsing what would be hundreds of thousands of individual Gaussian evaluations into a handful of convolutions (typically only ~9 groups needed even when the largest/smallest FWHM ratio in the pattern is 100). The convolution itself is done three different ways depending on the FWHM-to-step-size ratio (direct numerical summation, FFT, or — when the Gaussian is very narrow relative to the step size — an accurate analytic approximation built from exponential and top-hat convolutions). Reports a 300–1000x speed-up over alternative PDF-refinement programs, with the advantage growing as the number of atomic pairs increases. This is the algorithm behind TOPAS's `pdf_data`/`pdf_gauss_fwhm` refinement machinery described in `references/09-pdf-refinement.md` — distinct from the 2021 Coelho, Chater & Evans paper below, which addresses *generating* G(r) from raw reciprocal-space data rather than synthesizing/refining it once you already have G(r).

## Applications (Coelho as a contributing/co-author, not primary methodology)

These involve Alan Coelho as a co-author (contributing TOPAS-related analysis) on multi-author application papers, rather than as the originator of the core method. Included for completeness since TOPAS was used in the underlying analysis, but they're application results rather than TOPAS algorithm descriptions.

**DLSR: A solution to the parallax artefact in X-ray diffraction computed tomography data** — Vamvakeros, A., Coelho, A.A., Matras, D., Dong, H., Odarchenko, Y., Price, S.W.T., Butler, K.T., Gutowski, O., Dippel, A.C., v. Zimmermann, M., Martens, I., Drnec, J., Beale, A.M. & Jacques, S.D.M. (manuscript/preprint — no journal citation found in the extracted text).
In conventional ("reverse-analysis") XRD-CT, each sinogram is reconstructed into a real-space image first, and *then* a diffraction model is fitted per voxel — but the reconstructed patterns are lower quality than the raw projected ones, and in a large or thick sample the assumption that all X-rays scattered at a given 2θ along the beam path reach the same detector element breaks down (the parallax artefact), causing peak shift/broadening/splitting that scales as tan(2θ). DLSR (Direct Least-Squares Reconstruction) instead combines reconstruction and model-fitting into one nonlinear least-squares step: it solves a modified A·x = b system directly on the sinogram data, where A is the sparse ray-tracing coefficient matrix, x is the set of per-pixel physico-chemical models (nT×nT pixels, each with its own nonlinear diffraction model — phase scale factors, lattice parameters, crystallite sizes, etc.), and b is the raw sinogram stack — never forming an intermediate reconstructed image at all. (When the per-pixel model degenerates to a 0th-degree polynomial, this A·x=b system reduces exactly to the linear system used in ordinary tomographic reconstruction, showing DLSR as a generalization of it.) TOPAS was chosen as the nonlinear least-squares engine to solve this system specifically for its speed (C++), robustness, and ability to handle large data volumes. Demonstrated on both simulated data and a real phantom sample (SiC/TiO2 rutile/MgO powders in glass pipettes) built specifically to produce a parallax artefact, showing DLSR reconstructs correct local lattice parameters/crystallite sizes where reverse-analysis is corrupted by parallax — and, notably, shows that the common workaround of averaging patterns from opposing (φ, 180°+φ) tomographic angles only works for homogeneous samples and does not actually solve the parallax problem for heterogeneous ones.

**Imaging Heterogeneous Electrocatalyst Stability and Decoupling Degradation Mechanisms in Operating Hydrogen Fuel Cells** — Martens, I., Vamvakeros, A., ..., Coelho, A., et al. (2021). *ACS Energy Letters*.
Uses operando X-ray scattering tomography to map catalyst, carbon support, electrolyte and water distribution inside an operating hydrogen fuel cell, correlating device-scale degradation patterns with local chemical/thermal gradients.

**Holistic Multi-scale Imaging of Oxygen Reduction Reaction Catalyst Degradation in Operational Fuel Cells** — Martens, I., Vamvakeros, A., Martinez, N., Chattot, R., Pusa, J., Blanco, M.V., Fisher, E.A., Asset, T., Escribano, S., Micoud, F., Starr, T., Coelho, A., Honkimäki, V., Bizzotto, D., Wilkinson, D.P., Jacques, S.D.M., Maillard, F., Dubau, L., Lyonnard, S., Morin, A. & Drnec, J. (manuscript formatted in *Science* submission style — "One Sentence Summary"/"References and Notes" sections — but no confirmed final journal citation found in the extracted text).
A closely related/expanded study to the ACS Energy Letters paper above, providing device-level assessment of platinum cathode catalyst degradation using the same operando X-ray scattering tomography approach, extended to simultaneously map the catalyst, carbon support, polymer electrolyte, and liquid water distribution together so degradation can be correlated across all cell components at once, not just the catalyst.

**Energy-dispersive diffraction studies of inert anodes** — Scarlett, N.V.Y., Madsen, I.C., Evans, J.S.O., Coelho, A.A., McGregor, K., Rowles, M., Lanyon, M.R. & Urban, A.J. (2009). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 42, 502–512.
Develops a quantitative phase analysis method for energy-dispersive synchrotron diffraction data (via Rietveld refinement) and validates it against standard materials, finding it comparable in accuracy to conventional angular-dispersive diffraction. Applied to a tomographic study of molten-salt electrochemical cells using Magnéli-phase (Ti_nO_2n-1) material as a model inert anode for titanium production, tracking a CaTiO3/titanate scale layer forming on the electrode surface; while absolute detection limits weren't established quantitatively, the phase changes tracked scaled linearly with electrolysis time. Preparatory work for later in-situ studies of the same cells during actual operation.

**Mo2P4O15 — the most complex oxide structure solved by single crystal methods?** — Lister, S.E., Radosavljevic Evans, I., Howard, J.A.K., Coelho, A. & Evans, J.S.O. (2004). *Chem. Commun.*, 2004, 2–4.
Reports the crystal structure and phase transitions of Mo2P4O15, notable for having 441 crystallographically unique atoms in its asymmetric unit — at the time, the 5th-largest asymmetric unit of any inorganic structure in the ICSD, and the most complex commensurate *extended oxide* structure known. The previously published structure (Costentin et al. 1992) used a small subcell (a≈8.3, b≈6.5, c≈10.7 Å) but only achieved a low R-factor by invoking disorder and unusual ADPs; single-crystal data revealed superstructure reflections requiring a supercell 21x larger by volume (space group Pn, a≈24.1, b≈19.6, c≈25.1 Å), 42 formula units, and 441 unique atoms. Solved using the same whole-profile simulated-annealing methodology as Coelho (2000) above (see "Minimization and computational performance"): restraints held the 84 PO4 groups as rigid tetrahedra and the 42 MoO6 groups as distortable octahedra, simulated annealing refined all atomic coordinates plus one overall temperature factor against a limited data range from ideal starting positions, then repeated rounds of random perturbation (up to 0.1 Å) and re-refinement were used to escape false minima. Final free refinement against the whole dataset gave R=3.49% with isotropic ADPs — a concrete, unusually large-scale real-world validation of TOPAS's simulated-annealing structure-solution machinery on a genuinely hard superstructure problem, rather than a synthetic test case.

## Software architecture

**The TOPAS symbolic computation system** — Coelho, A.A., Evans, J., Evans, I., Kern, A. & Parsons, S. (2011). *Powder Diffraction* 26(S1), S22–S25.
Describes the symbolic-computation engine integrated into TOPAS that lets models be defined at run time using ordinary mathematical notation (rather than requiring source-code changes), evaluating only what has changed at the symbolic-equation-node level to keep this fast. This is the architectural basis for TOPAS's equation system, macros, and the `.inc` macro libraries bundled elsewhere in this skill. Concrete numbers: roughly 100 simplification rules are applied at the equation-node level, running at over 300,000 equations/second on a 2 GHz PC. A real stress-case example: a refinement with 2707 symbolic equations / 22,941 nodes / 276 independent parameters — which would expand to 1.7 billion nodes without the "recalculate only if necessary" mechanism — still converged in 6 iterations / 6.1s total, with symbolic evaluation accounting for only ~20% of that time (typically <10% in an ordinary Rietveld refinement, though up to 100% when only penalties are being minimized, since there's no Ycalc to dominate the cost). Architecturally, up to three operations are inlined per function call via 315 template-generated C++ functions, keeping the symbolic evaluator within ~2x of hand-coded speed for a single Pseudo-Voigt fit. (Note: the companion 2018 TOPAS-Academic overview paper below mis-cites this paper's year as 2007 in its own reference list — that's a typo in that paper, not an error here; 2011 is correct.)

## Software architecture and optimization (2018 J. Appl. Cryst. series)

**TOPAS and TOPAS-Academic: an optimization program integrating computer algebra and crystallographic objects written in C++** — Coelho, A.A. (2018). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 51, 210–218.
An overview paper describing TOPAS/TOPAS-Academic's functionality and internal architecture: a nonlinear least-squares optimization engine written in C++ that uses computer algebra to handle linear and nonlinear parameter constraints, with parameter dependencies for derivatives determined automatically rather than hand-coded. Also covers the conjugate-gradient solution method and how restraints/penalties are defined through the same computer-algebra system used for ordinary equations. This is the closest thing to a single architectural reference for how TOPAS as a whole fits together. Spells out the exact mechanical difference between the `restraint` and `penalty` keywords: restraints get their off-diagonal A-matrix terms calculated (unless `approximate_A`/BFGS is active) and often converge in fewer iterations, and are meant for "minimize (a-c)^2"-style problems; penalties always have their off-diagonal terms set to zero and are needed whenever the minimized function isn't naturally a square (including functions that can go negative). The objective function decomposes as σ²=σ0²+σP²+σR² (data + penalty + restraint contributions), with `penalties_weighting_K1` (typical range 0.1-2) as the overall weight on the combined penalty/restraint term and internal KP/KR factors that auto-balance them against the data term — this is the mechanism directly behind the `pen_weight`/`Pen_Wt` macro. Other concrete facts: TOPAS's kernel has over 600 keywords; threading gives ~2-4x speedup on a 4-CPU machine; computer-algebra overhead is typically only 5-10% of total runtime in ordinary refinements; and memory is managed via a "shadow object" scheme specifically designed to avoid reference-cycle-style leaks, letting large refinements free gigabytes of memory per iteration.

**Optimum Levenberg–Marquardt constant determination for nonlinear least-squares** — Coelho, A.A. (2018). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 51, 428–435.
Addresses a practical convergence problem in the Gauss–Newton method: sometimes solving the normal-equations matrix does not actually lower the objective function, typically because the objective function is near a minimum or the matrix is ill-conditioned. The usual fix is the Levenberg–Marquardt method, which adds a damping constant to the matrix diagonal; this paper introduces a method for determining a good approximate value for that constant automatically, improving convergence rate for both powder and single-crystal refinements. Quantified improvement: roughly a 10% reduction in computation for typical crystallographic refinements, up to 50% for large refinements. The new scheme mostly reduces to the old standard formula when the objective function is close to quadratic (negligible difference on NIST reference datasets and the Rosenbrock test function) — the real gains are concentrated specifically in problems using the BFGS-approximated A matrix (`approximate_A`), improving convergence rate there by 1.3-2.1x, which is exactly the regime TOPAS uses for large refinements — worth knowing when deciding whether this optimization is likely to matter for a given script.

**Deconvolution of instrument and Kα2 contributions from X-ray powder diffraction patterns using nonlinear least squares with penalties** — Coelho, A.A. (2018). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 51, 112–123.
Introduces a deconvolution method — tolerant of noise and not requiring the number of Bragg peaks to be known in advance — for removing instrument and emission-profile (e.g. Kα2) distortions from lab X-ray powder patterns. Producing a higher-resolution pattern this way makes it easier to identify which peaks are genuinely present and determine their true shapes, ahead of further analysis. This underlies the manual's Deconvolution chapter and its `Deconvolution_Bkg_Penalty`(Q_b)/`Deconvolution_Intensity_Penalty` macros. Concrete default constants: Q_p=0.5 "seems sufficient" for the intensity penalty; Q_b typically 0.1–0.5 for the background penalty, but must be reduced below 0.5 specifically when there are large 2θ regions with no background-only stretches (i.e. heavily overlapped patterns) — otherwise the fitted background can exceed the observed data. The bound-constrained conjugate-gradient solver from the 2005 paper above scales ~20N_p·N versus N^3 for a direct Newton/Cholesky solve — a claimed >100,000x speed gain at N_p=15,000 — plus a further ~20x from building the (constant, since all refined quantities are linear) A0 matrix only once, together keeping the whole deconvolution under a minute. A validation run on PbSO4 shows Rietveld refinement of the deconvoluted pattern reproduces essentially the same structural/background parameters as refining the raw pattern directly — i.e. deconvolution doesn't distort the underlying structural model. The paper explicitly flags PDF generation as a downstream application of this same method, foreshadowing the 2021 PDF-generation paper below.

**Whole powder pattern modelling macros for TOPAS** — Scardi, P., Azanza Ricardo, C.L., Perez-Demydenko, C. & Coelho, A.A. (2018). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 51, 1752–1765.
Describes a set of TOPAS macros implementing the whole powder pattern modelling (WPPM) approach, in which size- and strain-broadening contributions to the diffraction line profile are convolved with the standard instrumental profile already available in TOPAS, letting WPPM-style microstructural analysis be done using ordinary TOPAS macros (distributed as `WPPM_macros.inc`, built into TOPAS 7+ via `Topas.inc`) rather than a separate dedicated program — see also `references/20-miscellaneous.md`'s WPPM/`WPPM_ft_conv` material and the microstructure-convolution equations there. The specific macros introduced: `WPPM_Sphere_LogNormDIST(muc, muv, sigc, sigv)` for spherical-domain size broadening with a lognormal diameter distribution; `WPPM_Strain_InvariantWilkens` for Krivoglaz–Wilkens dislocation-based strain broadening (refines an average dislocation density ρ and an outer cut-off radius R_e — physically sensible bounds are ρ<0.1 nm⁻² and R_e between 0.1 nm and the largest domain size, e.g. 500 nm as a practical ceiling; the technique isn't sensitive below ρ≈0.0001×10¹⁸ m⁻²); `WPPM_Strain_InvariantPAH` for a more general phenomenological anisotropic-microstrain model applicable to any crystal symmetry via Popa's symmetry-allowed invariant polynomial (with `getInvariant`/`Out_Invariant` helper macros that derive/report that polynomial directly from the space-group number); and `WPPM_SR_Sphere` for surface-relaxation-induced peak asymmetry in spherical nanocrystals, notable as the first WPPM implementation of peak asymmetry via the imaginary component of the strain Fourier transform. Worked examples in the paper (reusable as script templates): ball-milled CaF2 with the dislocation-based strain model plus lognormal size distribution (~10% better Rwp/GoF than TOPAS's standard double-Voigt profile); ball-milled CaCO3 (calcite→aragonite) using the more flexible PAH model; and a Pt-nanocrystal surface-relaxation case cross-validated against a molecular-dynamics/Debye-scattering-equation simulation.

## PDF (pair distribution function) methods

**Generating the atomic pair distribution function without instrument or emission profile contributions** — Coelho, A.A., Chater, P.A. & Evans, M.J. (2021). *J. Appl. Cryst.* 54, 444–453.
Describes the method — implemented in TOPAS and TOPAS-Academic — for producing a sharper atomic PDF by removing instrument contributions (such as Kα2 from lab sources, or peak asymmetry from neutron time-of-flight data) before the real-space transform, rather than modeling them as part of the PDF itself. The sharper result makes structural parameters easier to identify. This is the primary methodology paper behind the manual's PDF-Generation chapter and the `decon.inp`-style worked examples (`Include_PDF_Generate`, `PDF-Generate.INC`). The central trick that makes the deconvolution well-posed: rather than fitting the reciprocal-space background with an unconstrained polynomial (which could absorb real coherent-scattering features), the background is constrained to a physically-motivated model — a scaled empty-capillary scan, `Capillary × Poly_Capillary + Poly_Fluorescence`, with those polynomials typically only first-degree — while peaks are fitted at every data point; a fit is judged successful when R_p<1%. An E(r) correction term (approximated as e1·cos(e2·r+e3)/r) corrects small violations of F(Q=0)=0; a large R_p improvement from including E(r) is itself a diagnostic that F(Q=0) was wrong. States a real limitation: the deconvolution has little benefit for near-amorphous materials with crystallite size below ~3 nm, since peaks are already broad enough that instrument/emission-profile contributions are negligible by comparison. Validated against GudrunX (fullerene C60) and shows concrete Rp behavior vs. r_max for tungsten/LiFePO4/Si comparing full vs. deconvoluted peak shapes (e.g. LiFePO4: Rp stays flat ~13-15% with increasing r_max for the deconvoluted case but rises sharply, 16.8%→19.3%, for the undeconvoluted case) — illustrating that the benefit of deconvolution grows specifically at high r.

## Protein structure solution

**Ab initio structure solution of proteins at atomic resolution using charge-flipping techniques and cloud computing** — Coelho, A.A. (2021). *Acta Cryst.* D77, 98–107.
Shows that large protein structures at atomic resolution can be solved in minutes by running charge-flipping across hundreds of virtual machines on Amazon Web Services at low cost (concrete figures: ~$0.11 USD to solve PDB 1cku using 200 VMs for 80s; ~500 spot VMs at ~$0.025/VM/hour). Cloud computing here isn't just an implementation convenience — the paper frames it as co-equal in importance to the algorithmic changes, since the fast (10-20 minute) turnaround per trial is what allowed the two new strategies (S0/S1, see `references/19-solving-proteins-atomic-resolution.md`) to actually be discovered and tuned interactively in the first place. Named specific mechanisms behind those strategies: Perturb-Symm (a space-group symmetry restraint on the electron density, default strength 0.25 — widens the radius of convergence at the cost of narrowing the space of solutions actually searched); Perturb-Atom (periodically boosts a randomly-chosen top-N intense electron-density peak 10x every 50 iterations, nudging the search toward a true heavy-atom position); Weak-90 vs. Weak-R (a systematic +90° phase offset vs. independent randomization of weak-reflection phases — Weak-R generally outperforms Weak-90); and a dedicated uranium-atom-solution-avoidance flip rule with a tunable threshold (~0.5 typical). States an explicit limitation: PDB entry 1w7q, a light-element-only protein (C/O/N/H, space group P65), was not solved by any strategy in the paper — a genuine boundary condition, not just a caveat. PDB 2knt specifically required combining the 2007 tangent-formula charge-flipping method (above) with the new S1 strategy to succeed, a concrete example of these two papers' methods being used together in practice.
